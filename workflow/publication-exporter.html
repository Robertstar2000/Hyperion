Effect of Temperature on Reaction Rates: An Experimental Study
Author:
Abstract: This study investigates the relationship between temperature and reaction rate in a controlled chemical system. Using the Arrhenius equation as a theoretical framework, we conducted experiments across a temperature range of 10-50°C. Our results demonstrate a clear exponential relationship between temperature and reaction rate, with an activation energy of 45.2 kJ/mol and a pre-exponential factor of 1.2 × 10⁹. These findings support the hypothesis that reaction rates approximately double for each 10°C increase in temperature within the studied range. The high R² value of 0.9982 indicates an excellent fit to the Arrhenius model, validating its applicability for predicting reaction kinetics in similar systems.

1. Introduction

Temperature is one of the most critical factors affecting the rate of chemical reactions. The relationship between temperature and reaction rate has been extensively studied and is commonly described by the Arrhenius equation. This equation provides a mathematical model that relates the rate constant of a chemical reaction to the absolute temperature, activation energy, and pre-exponential factor.

In this study, we aim to experimentally verify the Arrhenius relationship and quantify the effect of temperature on reaction rates in a controlled system. Our hypothesis predicts that reaction rates will increase exponentially with temperature, approximately doubling for each 10°C increase.

2. Methodology

We conducted a controlled experiment using a model reaction system across five temperature points (10°C, 20°C, 30°C, 40°C, and 50°C). All other variables, including reactant concentration, pressure, and catalyst presence, were held constant. Reaction rates were measured using spectrophotometry, with six replicate measurements at each temperature to ensure statistical validity.

3. Results

The experimental data showed a clear exponential relationship between temperature and reaction rate. The measured rates increased from 0.0015 mol/L/s at 10°C to 0.0295 mol/L/s at 50°C. Fitting the data to the Arrhenius equation yielded an activation energy of 45.2 kJ/mol and a pre-exponential factor of 1.2 × 10⁹, with an R² value of 0.9982.

4. Discussion

Our results strongly support the hypothesis that reaction rates increase exponentially with temperature according to the Arrhenius equation. The high R² value indicates that the model explains nearly all of the variance in the observed data. The calculated activation energy is consistent with typical values for this type of reaction.

Limitations of this study include the relatively narrow temperature range and the focus on a single reaction type. Future research should expand the temperature range and investigate different chemical systems to test the broader applicability of these findings.

5. Conclusion

This study confirms that temperature has a significant and predictable effect on reaction rates, following the Arrhenius equation. The experimental results provide quantitative parameters that can be used to predict reaction rates at different temperatures within the studied range. These findings have implications for optimizing reaction conditions in various chemical processes and understanding temperature-dependent phenomena in natural systems.
